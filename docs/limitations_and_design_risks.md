# Limitations and design risks

## Purpose

This document records methodological assumptions, possible failure modes and design choices for a staged programme of regional genetic component follow-up in neurodegenerative disease.

The aim is to make the project more robust by identifying limitations early, separating exploratory from confirmatory analyses, and avoiding overinterpretation of association or convergence evidence.

## Design risks

| Design risk | Why it matters | Planned handling |
|---|---|---|
| Regional components may not reflect causal biology | Association between a component and disease or imaging does not establish mechanism | Treat components as pre-specified genetic indices; defer mechanistic and causal claims until replication, conditional analysis and external evidence support them |
| Imaging effects may be modest | Imaging-genetic associations are often small and sensitive to cohort structure | Prioritise pre-specified endpoints, independent replication, effect-size reporting and negative-result documentation |
| MRI metrics are biologically non-specific | Diffusion and susceptibility-sensitive measures can reflect multiple tissue properties | Interpret imaging results as tissue-sensitive rather than mechanism-specific unless supported by molecular or histological convergence |
| APOE-region components may reflect known APOE haplotype structure | APOE is a major Alzheimer’s disease locus and can dominate regional interpretation | Include explicit APOE genotype or haplotype conditioning in AD-region analyses where data permit |
| Molecular annotation may be diffuse | LD, regulatory complexity and incomplete annotations can distribute evidence across many genes | Use uncertainty-ranked annotation rather than single-gene claims |
| Results may be cohort-specific | Cohorts differ in ascertainment, ancestry structure, imaging protocol and phenotype definition | Distinguish internal extension from external replication and report cohort-specific limitations |
| Multi-omics integration may encourage post-hoc storytelling | Large annotation spaces can support selective interpretation | Pre-specify annotation sources, evidence tiers and escalation rules |
| Regional component analyses may appear redundant with GWAS, PRS or fine-mapping | Established approaches already address important parts of the inference problem | Position the components as intermediate triangulation objects, not replacements for established methods |
| Public materials may be mistaken for an empirical results release | A protocol repository can be misread as containing validated findings | State clearly that this repository contains protocol documents and synthetic examples only |
| Data governance constraints limit reproducibility | Genetic and imaging data are sensitive and often controlled-access | Provide synthetic examples and workflow templates; do not release protected individual-level data or private component scores |

## Interpretation principles

The project should be framed as a staged validation programme rather than a discovery claim.

Claims should be escalated only when evidence moves from association to replication, convergence, conditional refinement and, where appropriate, perturbational or causal testing.

Negative or non-replicating findings should be treated as informative boundary conditions rather than hidden failures.
