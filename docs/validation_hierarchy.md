# Validation hierarchy

## Purpose

This document defines the evidence hierarchy used to move from disease-prioritised regional genetic components to stronger biological hypotheses.

The hierarchy is designed to prevent overinterpretation. Association, convergence, mechanism and causality are treated as distinct levels of inference.

## Evidence tiers

| Tier | Evidence level | Interpretation allowed |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | Disease association only | Prioritised regional genetic component |
| 2 | Disease association plus imaging association | Candidate genetic-imaging liability axis |
| 3 | Independent imaging replication | Reproducible genetic-imaging axis |
| 4 | Molecular or cell-type convergence | Plausible biological liability axis |
| 5 | Conditional robustness against known lead variants | Potentially distinct regional signal |
| 6 | Fine-mapping or colocalisation support | Candidate variant, gene or molecular trait |
| 7 | Perturbational or experimental support | Candidate mechanism |
| 8 | Multiple causal-inference and experimental lines of evidence | Causal hypothesis, still bounded by assumptions |

## Interpretation rules

A component should not be described as mechanistic unless it shows convergence beyond disease association alone.

A component should not be described as causal unless there is appropriate evidence from conditional analysis, fine-mapping, colocalisation, causal-inference methods and/or perturbational follow-up.

Negative or non-replicating results should be retained as informative boundary conditions.

## Confirmatory versus exploratory analyses

Confirmatory analyses should use pre-specified components, pre-specified imaging endpoints and pre-specified covariate structures where possible.

Exploratory analyses may be used to generate hypotheses, but should be labelled clearly and should not be presented as independent validation.

## Escalation criteria

A component should only move to conditional or causal refinement if it shows at least one of the following:

- independent imaging replication;
- strong molecular or cell-type convergence;
- evidence of conditional robustness against known regional signals;
- biologically coherent convergence across more than one domain.

## Failure modes

Possible failure modes include:

- no imaging replication;
- component effects collapsing to known lead variants;
- diffuse molecular annotation without clear convergence;
- inconsistent directionality across cohorts;
- cohort-specific artefacts;
- ancestry or LD-reference dependence;
- overinterpretation of non-specific imaging metrics.
